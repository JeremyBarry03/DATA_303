What makes a movie financially successful, and does that success say anything about quality?

The Business of Movies

Movies are interesting because making a lot of money does not always mean a film was a good investment,a studio can spend a huge amount, bring in strong revenue, and still have a weaker return than a smaller movie with a much lower budget. That is the main idea we wanted to test with genre, year, budget, revenue, and IMDb rating.

Our starting question was about the connection between movie quality and financial success. We wanted to see if bigger budgets, higher revenue, or stronger ROI actually line up with better audience ratings.

Data and Analysis Process

The Data

We used movie-level data with budget, revenue, genre, release year, IMDb rating, and IMDb vote count, then we filtered the dataset to only include movies with more than 10,000 IMDb votes, which helped cut down on noise from movies with very small audiences.

The Process

After cleaning the data, we built Tableau views that compared ROI, revenue, budget, genre, country, release year, runtime, and IMDb rating. We kept the graphs that did the best job answering how movie economics connect to audience response.

Measuring Success With ROI

Revenue is useful, but it only tells us how much money a movie brought in. ROI is different because it compares that money to the movie's budget. That makes it a better way to compare small and large productions.

This matters because a high-revenue film is not automatically the best investment. A smaller movie can earn less overall and still perform better if it cost much less to make.

Profit Margins Vary by Country

Before focusing on genre, we also looked at production country. Movie economics can change depending on the market, so country gives another way to compare where films tend to be more or less profitable.

In the map, red countries have stronger average profit margins, while blue countries have lower or negative margins. This helps show that the financial side of movies is not the same everywhere.

The First Surprise: Smaller Genres Can Win

The genre chart gives one of the clearest results. Documentaries have the highest median ROI, which makes sense because they are usually much cheaper to make than big-budget genres.

This is where ROI is helpful. A high-cost action movie may need a huge audience just to cover its spending, while a lower-budget genre can be successful with a much smaller release.

The chart should be used as a comparison of financial efficiency, not popularity. It shows which genres tend to turn their budgets into the strongest returns.

The Market Has Become Less Forgiving

ROI also changes over time, the trend suggests that median ROI has fallen, which means the typical movie in the dataset may be getting less efficient financially.

This connects back to our main question because financial success is not fixed. If newer movies are less efficient than older ones, then spending more money is an even riskier way to chase success.

The Ratings Question

After looking at money, we wanted to check whether it connects to quality. If bigger budgets usually made better movies, the graph would show a clear upward pattern between money and IMDb rating.

The relationship is weaker than that as bigger budgets and higher revenues evidently do not automatically lead to higher IMDb ratings across genres.

The trendlines make this easier to see. A movie can be expensive or commercially successful without being rated much better by audiences, so financial scale and audience approval are not the same thing.

Quality Has a Runtime Sweet Spot

Runtime gives one more way to look at audience response. The view below suggests that movies around 95 to 115 minutes have the strongest concentration of higher ratings.

This does not mean runtime decides whether a movie is good. It just adds another detail to the bigger point: audience ratings depend on more than money.

What This Says About Cinema

The graphs show that movie economics are not just about scale. The genre ROI graph makes this clear because documentaries have the strongest return on investment. They are not the biggest money-makers overall, but their lower budgets make their returns much more efficient.

The budget and revenue graphs add another piece. A movie can look successful because it earns a lot, but the ratings graph shows that more money does not automatically mean better IMDb scores. A blockbuster can be financially large without being rated much better than a smaller movie.

The time, country, and runtime graphs add context. Median ROI changes by release year, profit margins vary lots by country, and runtime gives another clue about audience response. These views all point to the same idea: financial success and movie quality do not relate to each other in a simple way.

Overall, cinema is both a creative product and an economic risk. Revenue measures size, ROI measures efficiency, and IMDb rating measures audience response. Looking at all graphs together shows why the movies that spend the most are not always the best investments.
